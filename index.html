 Was D.C. Cultural Retreat

Mount Union college students join in multicultural retreat in Washington, D.C.

By Michael Mullally
Special to The Review

   ”I never dreamed that I would be here when i was a boy on a farm down in southwest Stark Country milking cows," Republican Ohio Congressman Ralph Regula said to Mount Union Students touring the Capitol building while on the multicultural retreat in Washington, D.C., during the college’s spring break.

   Regula, a 1948 Mount Union alumnus and long-time member of the board of trustees, spoke to students on the necessity to get involved in public service. He said when he retires after his 18th consecutive congressional term, he wants to speak to political science students about what the political world was really like.

    The 19 Mount Union students who attended the multicultural retreat traveled to Washington, D.C., by train. The retreat was organized by Randy Clark, assistant professor of communication, and Dawn Adams, assistant director of international student services.

    The five-day trip to the nation’s capital by members of Regula’s staff and a wreath-laying ceremony at Arlington National Cemetery.

    The ceremony was performed by Shinya Shiozaki, a junior middle childhood education major of Nishinoomote-shi, Japan; Michael Mullally, a junior political science major of Garfield Heights; Stephanie Dominick, a sophomore English major of Massillon; and Kelsey Jones, a sophomore psychology major of

Harmony, PA.

   ”Just being on of the few Americans who gets to be that close to the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier and knowing the ceremony honored the soldiers who gave their lives for this country was the best part,” said Jones.

    Students had the opportunity to tour the District of Columbia and the surrounding areas. The group purchased week passes for the Metro rail system, D.C.’s subway system, to get from place to place.

    Students visited many sites including the National Holocaust Museum, branches

    of the Smithsonian, the National Zoo, Georgetown, the National Mall, the White House, Ford’s Theatre, Chinatown, Arlington National Cemetery and many others.

    “When I visited the Holocaust museum, I almost cried when I watched the videos and some stops in the museum,” said Ahn Ngo, a sophomore accounting major of Hanoi, Vietnam.

    The visit was a new experience for everyone. Shiozaki, who is blind, had several tours set up specifically for him so he would be able to better experience what D.C. has to offer. The guides

    at the national zoo and the Holocaust museum had fur and used several models, which allowed Shiozaki to learn about the museum and the animals.

    Some students saw home in D.C. “When I visited Georgetown, I was so surprises. It’s really like Hanoi,” said Ngo.

Academic Writing

Academic Writing

Throughout my academic career most of my research was spent in Political Theories and Ideologies. I also spent a few years focusing on political bias in media and advertising. Part of my continued education was spent on Communication practices as well as historical analysis.

I have included 4 papers that focus on; Political Theory and Ideologies from the views of Georg Hegel, John Locke, John Rawls, and Karl Marx, Political Bias and Campaign Advertising, American History, and a Communication Criticism of President Reagan's Challenger speech. The political theory papers discuss ideology of each individual theorist. I focused on Negative Campaign Advertising in America at the University of Akron and examine the benefits and issues that arise when candidates start mud-slinging. At the University of Mount Union each student must complete a Senior Culminating Experience which is a research paper using unique research to examine a topic, this paper focuses on the perceived bias of American news stories. During my education it was important that an in-depth study of American history take place, the next paper to read is an analysis of the removal of Native Americans under the "Indian Bill." The final paper available below analyses the speech by President Reagan after the Space Shuttle Challenger exploded over Cape Canaveral, Florida in 1986.
